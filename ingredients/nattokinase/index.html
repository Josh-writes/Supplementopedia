Nattokinase

Enzyme

Overview

Nattokinase is a serine‑protease enzyme produced during the fermentation of soybeans with Bacillus natto (the bacterium that creates the traditional Japanese food “natto”). In nutraceutical form it is marketed primarily for its fibrinolytic (blood‑clot‑dissolving) activity, which may help maintain normal vascular function and support cardiovascular health.

Benefits

Clinical and pre‑clinical studies suggest that nattokinase can (i) reduce plasma fibrin‑ogen levels and enhance clot‑lysis, thereby supporting cardiovascular health and reducing risk of thrombosis; (ii) modestly improve blood pressure, likely through enhanced endothelial nitric‑oxide production and reduced arterial stiffness; (iii) support cerebrovascular circulation, which may aid cognitive function in older adults by preserving microvascular flow; (iv) exhibit anti‑inflammatory effects by down‑regulating NF‑κB and cytokine release, potentially benefiting metabolic syndrome; and (v) enhance fibrinolytic capacity without affecting platelet count, offering a safer alternative to traditional anticoagulants for low‑to‑moderate risk individuals. The bulk of evidence comes from randomized controlled trials (RCTs) of 2–6 months duration and meta‑analyses of ≥10 studies, with effect sizes ranging from small (BP – 4–6 mm Hg systolic reduction) to moderate (fibrin‑ogen reduction 15‑20 %).

How It Works

Nattokinase (NK) is a 28 kDa serine protease that preferentially cleaves fibrin at the α‑ and β‑chains, directly degrading fibrin clots (fibrinolysis) and accelerating endogenous plasminogen activation. It also hydrolyzes fibrinogen, reduces plasma viscosity, and modestly activates tissue‑type plasminogen activator (t‑PA) while inhibiting plasminogen activator inhibitor‑1 (PAI‑1). By lowering circulating fibrinogen and enhancing plasmin activity, NK reduces thrombus formation. Additional pathways include up‑regulation of endothelial nitric‑oxide synthase (eNOS) → increased NO → vasodilation, and suppression of the NF‑κB pathway → lower pro‑inflammatory cytokines (IL‑6, TNF‑α). These combined actions improve vascular compliance, reduce platelet aggregation, and may protect microcirculation.

Dosage

Most human trials have used 2,000–4,000 FU (fibrinolytic units) per day, typically divided into two doses (e.g., 2,000 FU in the morning and 2,000 FU in the evening). Lower doses (500–1,000 FU) are often used for blood‑pressure support, while higher doses (up to 10,000 FU) have been examined for acute thrombotic risk under medical supervision. The enzyme is acid‑stable; capsules are usually taken with food to minimize gastric irritation, though some formulations advise empty‑stomach intake for optimal absorption. For individuals on anticoagulants or with bleeding disorders, a reduced dose (1,000–1,500 FU) is recommended, with physician oversight. Duration in studies ranges from 4 weeks (short‑term safety) to 12 months (long‑term efficacy).

Safety & Side Effects

Nattokinase is generally well‑tolerated at ≤10,000 FU/day, with mild adverse events (gastro‑intestinal discomfort, mild headache) reported in <5 % of participants. Contraindications include active bleeding, recent surgery (<2 weeks), severe thrombocytopenia, or known coagulation disorders. Major drug interactions involve anticoagulants (warfarin, direct oral anticoagulants, heparin) and antiplatelet agents (aspirin, clopidogrel), where additive anticoagulation can increase bleeding risk. Caution is advised for pregnant or lactating women due to insufficient safety data. Individuals with soy allergy should avoid nattokinase derived from soy‑based fermentation. Routine monitoring of PT/INR is recommended when combined with anticoagulants.

Chemistry

Nattokinase (EC 3.4.21.107) is a single‑chain serine protease belonging to the subtilisin family. Its mature form consists of 275 amino acids (≈27 kDa) with a catalytic triad of Asp‑32, His‑64, and Ser‑221 (chymotrypsin‑like). The enzyme’s primary structure:
MDSLGKYY... (full sequence available in UniProt P19121). The enzyme’s IUPAC name is not assigned (as it is a protein). Key structural features include a β‑sheet core, a calcium‑binding site that stabilizes the active conformation, and an N‑terminal leader peptide removed during maturation. The enzyme exhibits optimal activity at pH 7–9 and 37 °C, retaining >70 % activity after 2 h at 60 °C, which underlies its stability in supplement formulations.

Sources & Quality

Commercial nattokinase is extracted from Bacillus natto cultured in soy‑bean fermentations. The fermentation broth is harvested, the enzyme is purified by ammonium‑sulphate precipitation, ion‑exchange chromatography, and spray‑drying to produce a powder with ≥2,000 FU/g. Some manufacturers produce recombinant nattokinase (rNK) using E. coli or Pichia pastoris expression systems to increase purity and reduce batch‑to‑batch variability. Quality‑grade supplements are required to meet GMP standards and contain <5 % heavy‑metal contamination (per USP <231>) and no detectable soy protein allergens (if purified). Third‑party testing (e.g., NSF, USP) ensures activity claims (FU) match label, and ensures absence of microbial endotoxins. Selecting products with transparent certificates of analysis (COA) and a stable, acid‑resistant capsule is recommended for optimal bioavailability.
